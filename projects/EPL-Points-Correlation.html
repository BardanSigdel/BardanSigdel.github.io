Data Exploration and Analysis Project using Python¶

Does Club Spending in Professional Football Have Direct Bearing on Success? Does Location?¶

Executive Summary¶

The English Premier League, as is referred to the current structure of the English First Division football (soccer), is the world's most popular footballing league. Having redesigned its model from the traditional league in 1992, the "Premier League" grew to become the biggest global brand in league football in subsequent years, overtaking the leagues in Italy and Spain in terms of popularity and global fanbase. As such, it is no surprise that the league boasts of an incredible concentration of investment and expenditure from financial consortiums and groups from all across the world. The past couple of decades in particular have seen a mouthwatering amount of investment flood into the league, of which the sources range from Emrati royals to Russian oligarchs. With most of the top clubs all comfortably having spent over a billion euros just in accumulated player transfer fees (the sum paid to the club a player is being acquired from), the scrutiny over the concentration of wealth in the league is ever-increasing.

Using the correlation analyses shown below, we can gather a fair insight into the drive that expenditure has provided clubs in gaining "success", measured by the total number of points a club has accumulated since the 1992/93 season. As expected, in exploring the correlation between individual club expenditure and point accumulation, we can conclude with an observed correlation coefficient of 0.924 that there is a strong positive correlation between club expenditure and club success. The other area of examination focused on the spread of points across cities in the UK, whereby we do also notice that there is a positive relationship between club location and perceived success. However, this examination may have grounds for further research as the correlation is likely spurious due to confounding factors and unexamined societal or financial mechanisms associated with various locations in the United Kingdom.

Research Questions¶

The research questions that will be used to explore the dataset and proposed analyses are as follows:

Research Topic 1: Is there observable correlation between a Premier League team's total spending and success achieved? Do we have a case for introducing caps on spending and wages like other sports have?¶
Research Topic 2: Is there any relationship between club location and its success in the league? Are certain locations primed for success?¶

Data Description and Sources¶

Dataset¶

The data used for the purpose of the analyses here draws from three separate datasets:

  • "premclubdetails.csv" : directory of all 50 Premier League clubs (92/93 - 22/23) and their primary details (location, top goalscorer, etc.)
  • "premspending.csv" : data for clubs' expenditures (on transfers), incomes and balances during the timeframe
  • 'premtotals.csv" : data for all clubs' performances (matches played, won, points accumulated, etc.) during the timeframe
Controls¶

To have a control for the measure of "success", we will use the total points earned as a direct yardstick for how successful a team has been during this timeframe. Since the data used for this analysis uses monetary values as a key independent variable, we also require a uniform understanding and measure of monetary values across the data.

Monetary Values¶

The final dataset provides a clean combined view of all clubs and the appropriate variables needed for examination of the listed research questions. The monetary values in the original datasets were in either billions or millions of euros; in order to clean the data for a uniform examination, all values were converted to millions of euros.

Null Values¶

In order to further clean the dataset, expenditure and information for Wimbledon AFC were considered null values. This is mostly due to publicly accessible data on Wimbledon's finances (and changes to club structure) being murky and the data source choosing to omit their expenditure information in the interest of analytical veracity.

Data Sources¶

The data used for exploring the research questions in this analysis are derived from two sources:

  1. Official Premier League website (premierleague.com)
  2. Transfermarkt.com
Dataset¶

The final dataframe consists of the following columns and associated values for each:

  • Club: Name of the Premier League club
  • Location: Premier League club's location/city within the UK
  • Income: Total income reported by the club in the timeframe
  • Expenditure: Total amount of money spent by the club on transfers in the timeframe
  • Points Position: Position of the club in the final count of all points accumulated in the timeframe
  • Played: Total number of matches contested by the club in the timeframe
  • Won: Total number of contested matches won by the club in the timeframe
  • Points: Total points accumulated in the league by the club in the timeframe

Categorical Variable¶

After introducing and organizing the dataset as well as dropping any unnecessary or null values, we can examine the data and general tendencies our variables exhibit before delving into the analysis.

The one categorical variable, Location, is a key part of the overall analysis as it will help determine whether or not these Premier League clubs tend to congregrate in certain locations. The graphical representation below (code D7, "Categorical Variable Examination: Location) of the categorical variable "Location", which provides the club's city or township listing, clearly demonstrates that a large majority of these clubs (20%) are based in London. For an analyst, this likely exposes the first area of potential discombobulation in the data that could affect the veracity of the analysis to answer the second research question. If this topic is to be further investigated in future analyses, the potential for corrrelation-causation fallacy or spurious correlation could be examined while accounting for the size of London clubs and their share of the overall "sucess" pot.

Analysis and Findings¶

  1. Expenditure Correlation:

A very direct way of establishing the hypothesized correlation between club expenditure and point accumulation is to run a correlation analysis and examine the resulting coefficient. The scatter plot below (Code AN1 Total Expenditure vs Total Points SCT) expresses the distribution of point accumulation against club expenditure in a graphical form, which itself is sufficient to notice a general positive trend. When further explored through the correlation analysis (Code AN2 and the following coefficient matrix in Code AN3), we can establish a very clear positive correlation coefficient of 0.925 between club expenditure and point accumulation. This can be considered extremely strong correlation across the sample size (which is essentially every single club in league history minus one). It should always be noted that correlation does not equal causation in most cases, however, and additional analysis would be warranted to examine further underlying mechanisms before concluding relationships with high degrees of confidence.

Conclusion¶

Overall, the analysis conducted here strongly suggests a case for a potential introduction of financial regulatory measures in the league as club spending has a very strong positive correlation with success. While this should not surprising to anyone who follows the league, the narrow distribution of "success" while measured through these lenses would dishearten most fans who wish to see a "fairer" league.

The question of location-based success, while yielding a fairly noticeable trend, would likely need to be studied closer by taking into account unexamined factors that contribute to successes at clubs in more favorable (major cities) locations. For example, while London clubs very evidently tend to do well, attributing a London club's location in the United Kingdom as a direct contributor to its success would be a rather spurious (and rushed) establishment of relationship.
